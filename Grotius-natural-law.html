Hugo Grotius and the minimalist theory of natural law

I was writing a thesis about the poet Andrew Marvell, and I wanted to try to clarify Marvell‚Äôs references to Grotius, particularly those in ‚ÄúThe Character of Holland‚Äù, a satire written during the first Anglo-Dutch war. As I read the longer of the two works, I was immediately drawn to a question that seemed to me much more fundamental and of wider applicability than the lawfulness of claiming rights over the sea: the basis, if one could be found, of natural law, assuming that such a thing could be said to exist.

Theories of natural law hold that humans are subject to some obligations and duties that exist independently of and prior to any ‚Äúpositive‚Äù laws, by which is meant laws enacted by human institutions, such as lawmaking bodies or absolute monarchs. For much of human history, natural law theories were based in religion: the law of nature was thought to derive its force and validity from the will (and command) of God. But what happens when we no longer believe in God? Can we still take natural law seriously?

Jeremy Bentham (1748‚Äì1832) insisted that positive law was the only kind of law that was binding or enforceable. His opinion has been very influential. In practice, we‚Äôre almost all believers in positive law now, most of the time. I‚Äôve long thought that, if you believe the Nuremberg trials were anything more legitimate than ‚Äúvictors‚Äô justice‚Äù ‚Äî merely the imposition of its will by the prevailing party on the defeated one ‚Äî you must effectively base that judgment on some idea of natural law, even if you call it something else. The thing I was most excited to discover in Grotius‚Äôs writings was a possible answer to that question. I couldn‚Äôt imagine writing about Grotius without devoting substantial attention to the theory of natural law. That gave rise to two problems, though. Then there was the other problem: the conception I had of Grotius‚Äôs theory of natural law had been questioned by historians of political thought, who had contested the idea, previously popular among legal theorists in particular, that Grotius had been a more-or-less disinterested philosopher or theoretician of legal principles who effectively founded the field of international law. I wanted to say something about the Grotian ‚Äúminimalist‚Äù theory of natural law. In Grotius‚Äôs theory, the natural law consists only of those fundamental precepts which are evident to all human beings by virtue of our rational nature (‚ÄòPreliminary Discourse‚Äô, VIII, Rights, pp. 86‚Äì7). Those precepts are binding on all people, including those who have a wholly erroneous, or no, idea of God (‚ÄòPreliminary Discourse‚Äô, XI, Rights, pp. 89‚Äì90). So, Grotius has arguably succeeded in putting forward a basis for law which is both antecedent to positive law and independent of divine command.

The precepts of natural law include the obligation that people should be bound by their agreements and the obligations that they have assumed towards others. It‚Äôs on this principle that the whole edifice of government and systems of law, starting with the law of nations (ius gentium), is founded. Grotius says that the law of nations is based on the (common) will of all, or at least of many, nations. Acknowledging that, the law of nature apart, there is hardly any law that is universally accepted, he notes that the law of nations is thought to differ from one part of the world to another. But broadly speaking, the law of nations is binding on nations because at one time everybody who is subject to it, or their predecessors, agreed to it and they‚Äôre obliged to stand by their agreements.
